1. 📝 SUMMARY
This coaching call covered the full spectrum of AI implementation from no-code prototyping to enterprise lead generation. Brandon showcased how Lovable is disrupting traditional UI/UX workflows by enabling rapid iterative design in minutes rather than weeks. Jake demonstrated a client assessment tool built through POC-first development, while Andrew detailed a divide-and-conquer pattern for long-form report generation. Patrick presented his survey analytics platform Lens, and Paul revealed a cost-effective lead qualification stack using Perplexity's Sonar Pro API. The group also explored emerging standards like MCP, debated model selection in WindSurf, and provided strategic guidance on niching, pricing, and technical interview preparation.

2. 💡 KEY INSIGHTS
Brandon argued that Lovable represents an existential threat to traditional UI/UX designers, enabling founders to iterate through design options A, B, and C in three minutes rather than waiting a full week for designer mockups. Jake noted that building functional POCs directly with AI tools has replaced Figma for client work, allowing customers to "kick the tires" immediately and provide feedback on working software rather than static designs. Andrew described a repeatable divide-and-conquer pattern for generating long reports: chunking the document structure, processing sections in isolation against source material, then refining and rejoining the outputs. Paul demonstrated that Perplexity's Sonar Pro API can handle sophisticated lead qualification and company research for approximately ten dollars per two thousand companies, offering a cheaper alternative to complex scraping infrastructure. Brandon emphasized that agency founders should target tech-enabled executives at funded startups who value speed over cost, integrating with their existing stack of Slack, Notion, and HubSpot rather than building standalone tools. Brandon also outlined an authority-building flywheel: solve specific problems for a niche, document the solutions publicly, and attract inbound clients who recognize their exact pain points in your work. Patrick shared his integration of Tavus AI avatars for conducting conversational surveys that feel like Zoom interviews, automatically transcribing responses back into structured data.

3. ❓ KEY Q&A
Sagar asked Jake how AI wrappers and POCs transition to production environments. Jake explained he treats initial builds as proofs-of-concept to validate direction with clients before investing in scalable architecture, noting that early client involvement creates alignment that prevents surprises at delivery.

Andrew asked which models perform best in WindSurf for coding tasks. Jake and Brandon recommended Claude 3.5 for stability and reliability, though Jake noted Claude 3.7 had exhibited some issues in his testing. Bastian added that Claude 3.7 excels at writing and formal communications despite being less predictable for code generation.

Patrick asked about webhook integrations for course platforms. Brandon advised focusing on Mighty Networks over Skool due to superior API flexibility and native commenting features, suggesting Patrick prioritize two pain points for course creators: capturing video testimonials at completion and conducting pulse-check surveys throughout the curriculum rather than only at the end.

Sagar asked how to create content for an AI agency without getting lost in technical jargon. Brandon advised abandoning tool-focused posts in favor of outcome-driven narratives targeting specific niches, using the framework of identifying a business with money, solving their tedious operational problems, documenting the solution, and letting the work attract similar clients.

Cyril asked for advice on upcoming technical interviews for junior developer roles. Brandon recommended practicing algorithm problems on AlgoExpert, focusing on easy to medium difficulty, and emphasized the importance of verbalizing thought processes during whiteboarding sessions rather than coding in silence.

Tom asked about automating image generation for newsletter content. Brandon suggested extracting archetypal symbols and subcategories from article text to generate contextually relevant images, treating it as a numbers game where multiple generations are reviewed to find the twenty percent that work well.
