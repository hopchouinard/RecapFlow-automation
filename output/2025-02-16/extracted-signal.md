## insights

- Developers are like "the gym guy who only trains chest" — technically strong but neglecting the marketing side that actually brings in deals.
- The moment you start publicly demonstrating what you can do, opportunities flood in — visibility is the multiplier on technical skill.
- A confused viewer leaves. Every section of a video must answer: what am I about to learn, and why should I stay for the next two minutes?
- The two core reasons for low retention: the viewer is uninterested, or they don't know what's coming next.
- Thumbnail and title must instantly communicate who the video is for and what action is being taken — ambiguous language (e.g., "CVs" vs. "resumes," no verb before "thousands of resumes") loses the audience before they click.
- The first 30 seconds of a video should: name the outcome, show a visual of the end result, and mention any free resources being given away.
- "So that" is a powerful phrase — always tie the feature/tutorial back to the viewer's desired outcome (e.g., "analyze resumes *so that* you find the perfect applicant in seconds").
- Go shallow first across multiple use cases to validate which resonates, then go deep on the winner — this is the engineer's approach to content.
- Adding a progress bar at the bottom of videos (inspired by Alex Hormozi) measurably improves retention by visually signaling how much time remains.
- Never give away source code or templates without capturing an email first — you're generating leads you can't follow up with otherwise (Brandon cited creator Mervin as a cautionary example).
- The LinkedIn "comment for the blueprint" strategy is a current cheat code: post a short demo, offer a free resource to commenters, and auto-respond with value + a conversation starter.
- Building a personal brand is higher-leverage than building a single product because every new idea or experiment still builds the same brand — no starting from zero each time.
- The closer your content is to money (e.g., "I made $5K using this"), the more people will pay attention — but this only works if you have authority/proof to back it up.
- Riding a rising trend (e.g., Smolagents, CrewAI) is more important than having the perfect opinion — write trends, not takes, when you have zero credibility.
- Clients don't care what framework you used; they care about the end result. CrewAI content attracts people who end up needing chatbots, automations, or consulting — the label is just the door.
- For partnership/revenue-share agreements, always negotiate a minimum monthly floor (e.g., $2K/month) plus a percentage above that threshold — never rely solely on lead flow you can't control.

## qa

**Q (Delvis):** Is it wise to make videos based only on CrewAI since that's the product you're selling, or should you cover N8N, Make.com, and other tools too?
**A (Brandon Hancock):** You get to choose your own adventure, but pick one and commit. Making CrewAI content builds authority that unlocks multiple income streams simultaneously: beginners want coaching, companies want implementation help, and startups may want to hire you directly. The personal brand stacks — every video builds on the last — whereas hopping between products resets to zero each time.

**Q (Vlad):** Is the AI Authority Accelerator just for the eight weeks, or is there something after the program so participants aren't left on their own?
**A (Brandon Hancock):** After the eight weeks, freelancer contacts (editors, etc.) are handed over so participants can continue independently. There will also be a mastermind-style continuation focused on business decisions — which deals to take, how to price, how to monetize the audience that's been built. The goal of the eight weeks is to have opportunities coming in by the end; the mastermind helps you navigate them.

**Q (Richard):** What do you suggest when I'm not sure if something will work — should I post it anyway as a "crusader" documenting the journey, or wait until it works?
**A (Brandon Hancock):** Documentary/vlog-style content is very hard to get views on without pre-existing authority. Without a track record (e.g., a Stripe screenshot showing $10K/month), it's difficult to demand attention. Outcome-driven videos perform better. If you do document a journey, you need to be a strong storyteller who constantly builds tension and curiosity — otherwise viewers have no reason to stay.

**Q (Alexandra Spalato):** I'm doing two live calls per week for a community owner with 40K subscribers under a revenue-share deal, but I'm getting almost no leads and the one client I got came from the community itself. What should I do?
**A (Brandon Hancock):** Renegotiate to include a minimum monthly floor (e.g., $2,000–$2,500/month for your time), with the revenue share applying only above that threshold. Frame it as a mutual win — you stay engaged because you're compensated, and he gets more value as the community grows. If he won't pay a floor, you're being used and should be willing to walk away, since the arrangement isn't generating meaningful deal flow anyway.

**Q (Christopher Marin):** Has anyone built a GPT assistant that encodes the video-structure advice you give — hook, outline, retention tips — so creators can self-critique before recording?
**A (Brandon Hancock):** It's on the roadmap for the AI Authority Accelerator. The plan is to build AI assistants for each stage of the content creation process so participants can get instant feedback without waiting for a coaching call.
