## insights

- **Thumbnails should sell the dream; titles should be searchable.** The thumbnail communicates the desired outcome visually; the title is how people find the video via search. They should complement, not duplicate, each other.
- **"Show don't tell" is the most important principle for video intros.** Showing the end result (a chart, a generated output, a finished workflow) in the first 10 seconds is far more compelling than describing it verbally.
- **A confused viewer clicks away.** Every element of the intro — hook, roadmap, visual — should eliminate ambiguity about what the viewer will get by the end.
- **"Free" is one of the most powerful words on YouTube.** Human psychology responds strongly to high value for no cost; saying "free" in hooks and thumbnails reliably increases engagement.
- **Ride trending waves even if the topic isn't your favorite.** One or two trend-riding videos (DeepSeek, Gemini, O3 Mini) can outperform eight normal videos combined. There is typically a 3-week window before the wave passes.
- **Code on screen is a necessary evil, not a selling point.** Viewers want to see the desired outcome (a chart, a report, a clean UI), not raw code. Show the before/after, not the implementation.
- **Changing a video title is a low-effort, high-leverage fix.** Brandon recommended changing underperforming titles to be more specific and searchable, then waiting ~8 hours to observe the impact on analytics.
- **Curriculum and course content should be priced at 5x your hourly rate**, because you are packaging years of expertise that will be consumed by many people simultaneously, not just billing for hours worked.
- **Don't launch a newsletter until your YouTube channel hits ~10k subscribers.** YouTube is both top-of-funnel and nurturing at scale; a newsletter only reaches existing subscribers and diverts time from content that could reach hundreds of thousands.
- **Thumbnail variety matters.** If all thumbnails look the same, returning viewers may think they've already watched the video and skip it.
- **Re-recording a video is sometimes better than editing it**, especially when the hook and structure need fundamental changes and the topic is still trending.
- **Emulating successful channels is legitimate strategy.** Study the top 3 videos on a competitor's channel, reverse-engineer the structure, and ask "how can I make this even more valuable?"
- **Pricing for agency/consulting work should flex with demand.** Raise prices when leads are flooding in; lower them when things slow down. It's not a fixed number.

## qa

**Q (Vlad):** In terms of editing, do you recommend showing the outcome in code or something more conceptual that shows the kind of outcome you might expect?
**A (Brandon):** Code is a necessary evil — people don't like seeing it. Show the desired outcome instead. If you can show a nice-looking chart, graphic, or actionable output, people respond much better. Show the before (raw data) and after (meaningful insight), not the implementation in between.

**Q (Vlad):** What's the difference between what you should have on the thumbnail versus the title? Does it have to be the same?
**A (Brandon):** It's actually better if they're not the same. The title should be searchable — how a human would find the video via search. The thumbnail should be outcome-driven — what is the result the viewer wants? Sell the dream in the thumbnail; let people search via the title.

**Q (Vlad):** What is B-roll exactly — is it a clip of the actual functioning code?
**A (Brandon):** Yes, it can be a screenshot or clip of code running, transitioning to the next screen, or any visual that entertains the eye. Alternatively, you can switch to a tool like Excalidraw and show a simple step-by-step roadmap. The second option is easier and doesn't require a video editor.

**Q (Alexandra):** Should I go for a revenue share or upfront cash for the course collaboration with the other creator?
**A (Brandon):** Upfront cash is strongly preferable. With rev share you get sucked into ongoing obligations and delayed payment. Charge upfront, deliver the work, and if they want more, charge again. You could also just sell the same course yourself and keep 100% of revenue.

**Q (Alexandra):** What price should I charge for creating course content for another creator's community?
**A (Brandon):** Don't charge your hourly rate — charge 5x your hourly rate. You're not just doing work; you're packaging expertise that will be consumed by hundreds of people. If it takes 10 hours of your time but benefits 200 students, you're effectively charging $50 per student, which is very reasonable.

**Q (Alexandra):** Should I start a newsletter now?
**A (Brandon):** No — pause the newsletter until you hit 10k YouTube subscribers. YouTube serves as both top-of-funnel and nurturing at scale. A newsletter only reaches your existing ~137 subscribers, while YouTube gives you access to 443k potential viewers. Focus all available hours on high-leverage content creation.

**Q (Alexandra):** Should I move toward longer, deeper videos published less frequently, like the Ben AI channel model?
**A (Brandon):** Yes, but build up to it. Start with a one-hour crash course on a topic like AI personal assistants, get a professional thumbnail and video editor, and treat it as a cornerstone video. Don't jump straight to two-hour omni-agent tutorials — those take 80+ hours and $600+ to produce well. Build the muscle first.

**Q (Orlando):** Should I replace the underperforming video or upload a new one?
**A (Brandon):** Re-record it completely from scratch with a new hook and structure, then set the old one to unlisted rather than deleting it. The topic (DeepSeek R1 in Make.com) is still trending, so act quickly.

**Q (Delvis):** What's the plan for this channel/community going forward?
**A (Brandon):** The community will split into two tracks. The YouTube/brand-building coaching will move to a new program called the "AI Authority Accelerator" hosted on Circle, with more structured modules and multiple weekly support sessions. The current channel will eventually be restructured solely for developers building full-stack AI applications.
